# Ghost in the Machine: Stylometry vs AI

Detecting AI Imitation Through Stylometric Forensics

## Abstract

Recent advances in Large Language Models (LLMs) have made it possible to generate text that closely imitates the style of famous authors. This raises an important question:

Can a machine reliably distinguish genuine human writing from AI generated imitations of that same author?

In this project, I investigate whether modern language models retain detectable stylistic artifacts even when explicitly instructed to mimic a specific human author. Using Charles Dickens and Jane Austen as case studies, I construct a carefully controlled dataset consisting of authentic passages and Gemini generated passages written in the specific style of each author.

To avoid common evaluation pitfalls such as topic leakage, chapter overlap, and dataset contamination, I perform strict book level train/validation/test partitioning and multiple random seed evaluations.

My results show that a lightweight DistilBERT classifier achieves approximately 96.6% accuracy and 99.7% macro AUC on completely unseen books, demonstrating that AI generated imitations retain a measurable stylistic fingerprint despite explicit attempts to emulate human writing.

Finally, I conduct an adversarial Turing Test Attack using evolutionary search and show that while the detector is highly robust, carefully optimized AI generations can occasionally evade detection.

## Motivation

The emergence of large language models has created a new challenge for authorship attribution.

Traditional authorship attribution asks:

Who wrote this text?

Modern AI introduces a different question:

Did a human write this text, or was it generated by an AI attempting to imitate that human?

Most existing AI detectors focus on generic human vs AI classification. However, these detectors often exploit superficial signals and fail when models are specifically instructed to imitate a particular author.

I wanted to investigate a more difficult and realistic scenario:

If Gemini is asked to write exactly like Charles Dickens or Jane Austen, can I still distinguish it from the genuine authors?

This transforms the problem from generic AI detection into a stylometric forensics task.

## Research Question

My central research question was:

Do large language models retain detectable stylistic fingerprints even when explicitly prompted to imitate a human author?

Formally:

Given a text passage, can a classifier reliably determine whether the passage is authentic or synthetic?

## Initial Hypothesis

I considered two competing hypotheses.

### Hypothesis 1
Gemini successfully reproduces the authors' styles.

If true:
* Human and AI passages become nearly indistinguishable.
* Classification accuracy should approach random guessing.
* Any detected differences would likely come from topic or content leakage.

### Hypothesis 2
Gemini only approximates the styles.

If true:
* Residual stylistic artifacts remain.
* These artifacts can be learned by a classifier.
* Classification remains possible even on completely unseen books.

This project was designed to determine which hypothesis better reflects reality.

## Dataset Construction

A major challenge in authorship studies is data leakage.

Many published results are inflated because train and test sets contain neighboring paragraphs from the same source text.

A classifier can then memorize local context instead of learning style.

To avoid this issue, I designed a stricter protocol.

### Human Corpus
Authentic texts from Charles Dickens and Jane Austen were collected from Project Gutenberg.

Examples include:
* Oliver Twist
* Great Expectations
* Pride and Prejudice
* Emma

Gutenberg metadata and boilerplate sections were removed.

This ensured the model could not rely on publisher specific artifacts.

### AI Corpus
For every authentic passage, Gemini was instructed to generate a stylistically similar passage in the voice of the target author (Dickens or Austen).

The objective was not paraphrasing.

Instead, Gemini was asked to:
* emulate the author's tone
* emulate the author's sentence structure
* emulate the author's vocabulary
* emulate the author's narrative style

while producing entirely new text.

This creates a difficult attribution setting because the AI is actively attempting to hide its identity.

## Preventing Leakage

I considered several forms of leakage.

### Chapter Leakage
If chapter one appears in training and chapter two appears in testing, the classifier may exploit recurring content.

To prevent this:
* books were partitioned before splitting
* entire books belonged to only one split

A test book never appeared during training.

### Topic Leakage
A model might learn that orphans imply Oliver Twist instead of learning Dickens' style.

Book level splitting substantially reduces this risk.

### Metadata Leakage
Gutenberg headers and footers were removed.

This prevents trivial cues from influencing predictions.

## Model Selection

I selected DistilBERT because:
* compact architecture
* efficient training
* strong text classification performance
* fewer parameters than full BERT

The goal was not to maximize benchmark performance.

The goal was to determine whether the signal exists at all.

If DistilBERT can separate the classes, then the stylistic signal is likely substantial.

## Experimental Design

To measure robustness, I conducted multiple independent runs.

For each seed:
1. Shuffle book assignments
2. Rebuild train/validation/test splits
3. Extract features
4. Fine tune DistilBERT from scratch
5. Evaluate on unseen books

This produces a distribution of results rather than a single lucky score.

## Results

Across five independent seeds:

* Seed 42: Accuracy 0.92, Macro AUC 0.993
* Seed 1337: Accuracy 1.00, Macro AUC 1.000
* Seed 2024: Accuracy 1.00, Macro AUC 1.000
* Seed 101: Accuracy 0.98, Macro AUC 1.000
* Seed 999: Accuracy 0.93, Macro AUC 0.992

### Aggregate Performance

Mean Accuracy: 96.6% ± 3.4%
Mean Macro AUC: 99.7% ± 0.37%

These results remained stable despite:
* different random initializations
* different train/test book assignments
* strict out of distribution evaluation

## Interpretation

The results strongly support Hypothesis 2.

Gemini successfully imitates many surface characteristics of Dickens and Austen.

However, the imitation is not perfect.

The classifier consistently identifies residual differences between:
* genuine text
* Gemini attempting to be the author

Importantly, because evaluation occurs on entirely unseen books, the classifier cannot rely on memorized content.

It must be exploiting deeper stylistic patterns.

## Adversarial Evaluation

High accuracy alone does not prove robustness.

An intelligent attacker may actively optimize text to evade detection.

To test this scenario, I designed a Turing Test Attack.

### Evolutionary Search

A genetic algorithm was used to search for passages that maximize the probability of being classified as human under the trained classifier.

Each generation:
1. Produces candidate passages
2. Evaluates detector confidence
3. Selects elite survivors
4. Mutates stylistic characteristics
5. Repeats

This simulates an adversary attempting to fool the detector.

## Findings

Surprisingly, the initial generation already produced an example with a 65.08% probability of being classified as human.

Despite the detector achieving approximately 97% accuracy overall, an optimized Gemini sample immediately crossed the decision boundary.

This demonstrates:
* detector robustness on average
* detector vulnerability under adversarial optimization

The result mirrors adversarial examples in computer vision.

A classifier can be highly accurate while still admitting rare successful attacks.

## Key Takeaways

### 1. AI Imitation Is Detectable
Even when Gemini explicitly imitates Dickens and Austen, detectable stylistic artifacts remain.

### 2. Generalization Matters
The signal survives:
* unseen books
* different train/test partitions
* different random seeds

suggesting the classifier learns style rather than content.

### 3. High Accuracy Does Not Mean Perfect Security
Adversarial search can occasionally generate passages that evade detection.

### 4. Stylometry Remains Powerful
This work reinforces decades of findings in authorship attribution: small stylistic patterns often persist even when content changes.

## Conclusion

This project began with a simple question:

Can an AI truly write like a classic author?

The answer appears to be:

Not completely.

Even when explicitly instructed to imitate a human author, Gemini leaves behind detectable traces of its own generative process.

A relatively small DistilBERT classifier was able to identify these traces with approximately 97% accuracy on completely unseen books.

At the same time, adversarial search demonstrated that these fingerprints are not invulnerable.

The resulting picture is nuanced:

AI generated imitations are neither perfectly indistinguishable nor trivially detectable.

They occupy an intermediate space where stylometric fingerprints remain surprisingly strong, yet can occasionally be manipulated by a determined adversary.

This makes authorship attribution in the age of large language models not only possible, but an increasingly important area of research.
